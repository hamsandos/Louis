★ Locals Only ★
The Real Louisville — 10 More

These aren't on the tourist maps. They're the facades Louisville lives in — the buildings where locals eat, drink, create, argue about art, watch plays, and fall in love with their city all over again. No museum gift shops. No bourbon selfie stations. Just the buildings that make Louisville feel like home.

31
Locals' Icon
Actors Theatre of Louisville (Bank of Louisville Building)
1837 · Renovated 1972
One of the oldest surviving buildings in downtown Louisville — a tiny, perfect Greek Revival gem on West Main. Its modest stone columns and symmetrical facade hide one of America's most important regional theatres. Locals pack the annual Humana Festival of New American Plays every spring. This isn't a tourist stop; it's Louisville's creative heartbeat. The building whispers while the shows inside roar.
Greek Revival
📖 Wikipedia 🔗 Official Site 📸 Flickr 🗺️ Google Maps 📖 Clio
32
Locals' Icon
Troll Pub Under the Bridge (L&N Railroad HQ)
1877 · Reopened 2011
The subterranean remains of the old Louisville & Nashville Railroad headquarters, buried under Washington Street after the 1937 flood. Locals descend past Louie the 8½-foot troll into a cavernous stone cellar with original masonry walls. It was an artist colony in the '70s. Now it's where Louisville watches the Cards play, drinks bourbon old-fashioneds, and argues about who makes the best Hot Brown. The facade is literally underground — and that's the point.
Romanesque Commercial / Subterranean
🔗 Official Site 📸 Flickr 🗺️ Google Maps ⭐ Yelp Photos
33
Locals' Icon
KMAC Museum (formerly Museum Plaza)
c. 1890s · Renovated 2016
A restored Whiskey Row warehouse turned into Louisville's scrappy, adventurous contemporary art museum. The facade is understated brick with a sleek modern entry canopy — nothing showy, which is exactly the point. Inside, local artists get shown alongside international names. The rooftop bar is one of the best-kept secrets downtown. Louisvillians know this is where the real art lives, not behind velvet ropes.
Victorian Commercial / Adaptive Reuse
📖 Wikipedia 🔗 Official Site 📸 Flickr 🗺️ Google Maps
34
Locals' Icon
Mayan Cafe Building (NuLu / East Market District)
c. 1880s
A humble two-story brick storefront on East Market Street in NuLu — Louisville's arts district born from grit, not a developer's boardroom. Chef Bruce Ucán serves indigenous Yucatecan food with locally sourced ingredients inside. The facade is unassuming — faded brick, simple awning, small windows — but locals will tell you this is the most important restaurant building in the city. It represents what NuLu is: authentic, independent, rooted.
Victorian Commercial Storefront
🔗 Official Site 📸 Flickr 🗺️ Google Maps ⭐ Yelp Photos
35
Locals' Icon
Butchertown Grocery (Washington Street)
c. 1860s · Renovated 2016
A Civil War-era corner grocery building in Butchertown — Louisville's oldest meatpacking neighborhood — now home to one of the best restaurants in Kentucky. The narrow brick facade with its painted signage and original transom windows is the kind of building you'd walk past if you didn't know. But locals know. The adjacent bakery's Cruffins draw lines on Saturday mornings. This building is Butchertown's renaissance in a single frame.
Vernacular Commercial / Corner Store
🔗 Official Site 📸 Flickr 🗺️ Google Maps ⭐ Yelp Photos
36
Locals' Icon
Garage Bar (NuLu Service Station)
c. 1920s · Renovated 2012
A converted 1920s auto service station on Market Street in NuLu, with the original garage doors still intact — they roll up in warm weather so the inside and the patio become one space. Ping-pong tables, wood-fired pizza, local brews. The building doesn't try to be anything other than what it was, and that's why Louisville loves it. No pretense. Just good food in an honest old building with the doors wide open.
Early 20th-Century Commercial / Adaptive Reuse
🔗 Official Site 📸 Flickr 🗺️ Google Maps ⭐ Yelp Photos
37
Locals' Icon
Copper & Kings Distillery (Butchertown)
c. 1880s warehouse · Renovated 2014
In a city drowning in bourbon, Copper & Kings makes brandy — and that's the most Louisville thing imaginable. The old brick warehouse facade in Butchertown is topped by one of the best rooftop bars in the city, where locals gather for sunset views of downtown. The building's industrial bones — loading docks, heavy timber, iron hardware — are left exposed and proud. It's the rebel distillery in a conformist row.
Industrial Warehouse / Adaptive Reuse
📖 Wikipedia 🔗 Official Site 📸 Flickr 🗺️ Google Maps
38
Locals' Icon
Roots 101 African American Museum
c. 1860s · Renovated 2019
Housed in a quiet historic storefront on West Main Street, this small but deeply powerful museum tells the story of Black Louisville from the Middle Passage to today. The facade is modest brick with painted signage — easy to miss if you're rushing between the tourist museums. But locals know this is one of the most important buildings on the Row. It tells the story that the big museums often whisper.
Victorian Commercial Storefront
🔗 Official Site 📸 Flickr 🗺️ Google Maps ⭐ TripAdvisor
39
Locals' Icon
The Kentucky Center for the Performing Arts
1983
It's not pretty in a classical sense — the angular brutalist-modern facade of glass and concrete on the riverfront is polarizing. But locals love it anyway because it's where Louisville happens: the ballet, the orchestra, Broadway tours, community theatre, school recitals. It's the living room of the city's performing arts scene, and the lobby views of the river at intermission are worth the ticket alone.
Late Modernist / Brutalist
📖 Wikipedia 🔗 Official Site 📸 Flickr 🗺️ Google Maps
40
Locals' Icon
Falls City Brewing Building (NuLu)
c. 1905 · Revived 2010
Falls City was Louisville's beer before bourbon was cool again — the working-class lager that sponsored Friday night fish fries and little league teams. The original brewing heritage lives on in a restored industrial building in NuLu with exposed brick, steel trusses, and the old Falls City signage painted back on the exterior. Grabbing a can of Falls City on a hot afternoon while sitting on the patio is a Louisville rite of passage. The building is a love letter to the city's blue-collar soul.
Industrial Brewery / Adaptive Reuse
